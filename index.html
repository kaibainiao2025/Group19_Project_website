Abstract

Understanding plant root system architecture (RSA) is important for applications in sustainability, climate adaptation, and crop improvement. Minirhizotron (MR) imaging systems acquire high-resolution RGB images of roots through transparent tubes installed in the soil. This page introduces a large-scale MR image dataset spanning multiple plant species and growth conditions, with both image-level and pixel-level annotations to support weakly supervised and fully supervised root segmentation research.

The dataset includes tens of thousands of images covering variation in species, soil types, depths, and root structures. A substantial portion of the images is accompanied by binary masks labeling each pixel as root or non-root, enabling benchmarking of semantic segmentation algorithms for root detection and analysis.

Minirhizotron System

Example minirhizotron system: transparent tubes installed in the field allow a camera to capture root images at different depths over time.

Minirhizotron technology installs clear tubes into the soil at an oblique angle before planting. As plants grow, a camera is lowered along each tube to capture images of nearby roots at multiple depths. Because the tubes remain in place for the entire growing season, the same root regions can be imaged repeatedly to track development over time.

This setup enables non-destructive, longitudinal observation of RSA, including changes in root length, diameter, branching structure, density, and color as the plant responds to environmental conditions.

Dataset Summary

High-level summary of the dataset, including number of images, species, and annotation types.

  • Large-scale RGB dataset of plant roots captured using minirhizotron systems.
  • Multiple plant species with diverse soil conditions and tube depths.
  • Image-level labels indicating the presence or absence of visible roots.
  • Pixel-level binary masks for a large subset of images.
  • Designed to support both weakly supervised and fully supervised methods.

Manually Annotated Ground Truth Masks

Example pairs of RGB images and corresponding binary masks used as ground truth for semantic segmentation.

Pixel-level annotations label each pixel as belonging to the root system or the surrounding soil. These masks allow quantitative evaluation of segmentation algorithms using metrics such as IoU, Dice score, and pixel accuracy.
